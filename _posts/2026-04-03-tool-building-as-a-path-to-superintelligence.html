Paper Explainer Reasoning Agents · February 2026 · Koplow et al. · Poggio Lab, MIT · arXiv 2602.21061

Tool Building as a Path
to “Superintelligence”

The Diligent Learner framework promises that any model with a high enough per-step success rate can, in principle, solve arbitrarily hard problems via search. This paper tests that claim rigorously — and finds that the bottleneck is not scale or search depth, but the ability to use tools precisely.

Introduction

There is a compelling theoretical argument that LLMs could achieve superintelligence through test-time search alone — no new architecture, no extra training, just more inference-time computation. The argument, formalized in the Diligent Learner framework, rests on one key quantity: the per-step success probability $\gamma$.

The idea is elegant. If your reasoner is reliable enough at each individual step, you can stack steps arbitrarily deep. A model with $\gamma \geq 0.9$ per step is, in principle, capable of multi-hundred-step reasoning chains, provided it has a good search strategy and can backtrack on failures.

The core question Does $\gamma$ actually stay high as problems get harder? This paper provides the first rigorous empirical answer — using a benchmark specifically designed to make shortcuts impossible.

The result is nuanced and important: small models fail catastrophically as depth increases, while frontier models show surprising robustness — but only when they use tools to manage state externally.

The Diligent Learner framework

Reasoning as a search tree

The Diligent Learner formalizes multi-step reasoning as a depth-first search over a tree. Each node is a partial reasoning state. An LLM navigates the tree by emitting one of three operations at each step.

Fig. 1 — DFS over reasoning states. At each node the LLM emits "new node" (extend), "done" (solution found), or "backtrack" (reject and retry). The gold dashed branch shows a failed path being pruned and retried.

Under this formalism, the quality of a reasoner is captured almost entirely by $\gamma$. Two mild assumptions complete the theory: $\gamma$ must be bounded away from zero, and a validator can distinguish correct from incorrect partial chains. Under these conditions, DFS with backtracking is both sample-efficient and complete.

The gap in the theory

The framework's Achilles heel is the first assumption. If $\gamma$ degrades superlinearly with depth — each new step becoming exponentially harder — the search tree explodes and the whole argument breaks down. No one had rigorously tested whether this happens in practice.

The original Diligent Learner (Shalev-Shwartz & Shashua, 2025) proved the framework works under the $\gamma \geq \gamma_0$ assumption. This paper provides the first systematic empirical measurement of whether that assumption holds on out-of-distribution logical inference.

Why existing benchmarks miss the point

Most reasoning benchmarks do not isolate $\gamma$. They either score only the final answer, admit many valid intermediate paths, or are solvable by exploiting dataset-specific shortcuts. To measure $\gamma$ cleanly, you need a task with four strict properties:

1
Unique extensions at each depth.
Exactly one correct next step must exist. Multiple valid paths let a model succeed by accident, masking its true $\gamma$.
2
Full history is informationally required.
The correct extension must depend on all prior context. If recent evidence alone suffices, the model can ignore history and still look competent.
3
Shortcuts are information-theoretically impossible.
Ignoring accumulated history must provably prevent success. Pattern-matching to training distribution should offer no help.
4
Scalable to arbitrary depth.
The task must be easy to generate at any depth, so $\gamma$ can be measured as a continuous function of chain length.

No standard benchmark satisfies all four. The authors built one from scratch.

The GF(2) circuit reconstruction benchmark

The benchmark is built around Boolean circuits over $\mathrm{GF}(2)$ — the field with two elements, where addition is XOR. A hidden circuit takes a binary input and produces a binary output. The LLM is shown input/output examples one at a time and must reconstruct which circuit produced them. Each depth step reveals one more sample.

Fig. 2 — The stepwise GF(2) reconstruction task. Each depth adds one sample; the hypothesis space |H| halves. Every prior constraint must be fused at every step.

The algebraic structure of $\mathrm{GF}(2)$ guarantees exactly one correct consistent extension at each depth. At depth $d$, the remaining hypothesis space is $|\mathcal{H}_d| = 2^{\dim\ker(A_d)}$, where $A_d$ is the constraint matrix of accumulated samples. Any model that fails to maintain $A_d$ accurately cannot reliably extend correctly.

What the paper found

Finding 1 — Small models collapse superlinearly

For small language models, $\gamma$ does not degrade gracefully. It falls superlinearly with problem depth — each additional step becoming disproportionately harder than the last.

$$\gamma_{\text{small}}(d) \approx \gamma_0 \cdot e^{-\alpha d^2}, \quad \alpha > 0$$

This is the critical finding. If $\gamma$ falls superlinearly, the search tree explodes faster than any reasonable compute budget can cover. No amount of test-time scaling can compensate. The analysis traces this to a concrete failure mode: small models behave as partial-information estimators — accurate on recent evidence, amnesiac on history.

Fig. 4 — In-context memory (left) vs. external tool state (right). The small LLM compresses and forgets; the tool-augmented model preserves every constraint with equal fidelity.

Finding 2 — Frontier models show partial robustness, but only with tools

State-of-the-art frontier models exhibit substantially more stable $\gamma$ — but this robustness is contingent on their ability to make precise tool calls to manage state externally. Without tools, even frontier models show non-trivial $\gamma$ degradation at depth.

Key result
A model that offloads state tracking to an external tool is not relying on its own working memory to hold the accumulated history. The model's job becomes narrower — call the right tool at the right time — and that is a much more tractable problem.
“Progress toward superintelligence depends less on scaling test-time compute or deepening search, and more on architectures that can build and use tools precisely.”

Finding 3 — Tool design quality is the critical variable

Among tool-using frontier models, the single largest predictor of $\gamma$ stability is the quality of the tool design — not model size, not context length, not base reasoning ability. Precise, well-specified tools that match the task structure allow the model to maintain $\gamma$ over long sequences. Poorly designed tools cause failures even in the strongest models.

This identifies tool design as a first-class research problem — not just an engineering convenience, but a core capability determining whether the Diligent Learner hypothesis holds in practice.

What this does and does not claim

The GF(2) benchmark is adversarially designed — a worst case for state maintenance where there is no slack, no shortcuts, and no forgetting allowed. Many real-world tasks are easier. The benchmark isolates a failure mode, not a universal limitation.

The finding about frontier models is "partial robustness," not "solved." Even the best tool-using models show some $\gamma$ degradation at sufficient depth. Whether $\gamma$ can be kept bounded away from zero over arbitrarily long chains remains open.

What this supports
The Diligent Learner framework can hold in practice for frontier models with precise, well-designed tools, at least over the depths studied.
?
What remains open
Whether $\gamma$ stays bounded at much larger depths, whether tool-building generalizes to novel task classes, and how tool quality scales with autonomy.
Takeaway

The Diligent Learner framework offers a principled path to superintelligence through search — but it requires $\gamma$ to remain high as problems get deeper. This paper tests that claim rigorously for the first time.

For small models, $\gamma$ collapses superlinearly. They behave as partial-information estimators that cannot maintain full accumulated state. Test-time compute scaling cannot rescue them on this class of task.

For frontier models, $\gamma$ stays more stable — but only when they use tools to offload state management externally. The quality of tool design is the single strongest predictor of $\gamma$ stability across depth.

If the Diligent Learner hypothesis is right, progress toward general superintelligence depends less on scaling parameters or search depth, and more on architectures that can construct and use tools precisely.
