## Introduction

Deep learning works extraordinarily well in vision, language, and audio. In those domains, it can feel as though we already have a broadly applicable learning recipe. But outside these regimes there are many simple tasks that standard training methods still do not handle well: primality testing, parity, Boolean circuits, graph connectivity, and other rule-based problems. One can cast them as supervised learning tasks and train a large model on labeled examples. In practice, the model often fits the training data and still fails to recover the underlying rule.

A natural alternative is to use the fact that many such tasks admit short exact implementations, often just a few lines of Python. Instead of training a model to imitate the input-output map, we can search directly for a program that explains the data. This is empirical risk minimization (ERM) over a discrete class of programs, and classical learning theory tells us that such an approach can generalize from surprisingly little data.

The difficulty is computational. Exhaustive search over all Python programs is clearly infeasible. The number of candidate programs grows exponentially with program length, so even when the correct hypothesis is very short, brute-force ERM is usually not practical.

This leads to the central question of the paper: can one retain the sample-efficiency of program-based ERM without paying the full cost of uniform search?

In full generality, the answer is no. There are genuine computational-statistical tradeoffs here. But one can still do much better than exhaustive enumeration. The main idea behind LLM-PV is to use a pretrained LLM as a search prior. The LLM does not solve the learning problem by itself. Rather, it biases the search toward short, plausible programs, making ERM-style selection practical when the model assigns sufficient probability mass to good candidates.

<div class="key-message">
  Pretrained LLMs can serve as search priors for empirical risk minimization over short programs.
  This makes classical program-based ERM practically searchable while preserving its strong sample-efficiency advantages.
</div>

The story has four parts:

<ol style="margin: 0; padding-left: 0; list-style-position: inside;">
  <li><strong>Deep learning fails on program-like tasks.</strong> SGD-trained networks can fit the training data and still fail to recover the underlying rule.</li>
  <li><strong>ERM over short programs is sample-efficient.</strong> PAC-style guarantees scale with description length, not ambient input dimension.</li>
  <li><strong>Brute-force ERM is computationally infeasible.</strong> Naive enumeration over programs is exponential in program length.</li>
  <li><strong>LLM-PV changes the search procedure.</strong> A pretrained LLM provides a structured proposal distribution over candidate programs, making ERM-style selection feasible in practice.</li>
</ol>

## Part I: Deep learning can be the wrong search procedure

### Where our usual intuition comes from

Most of our intuitions about sample efficiency come from settings where deep learning is well matched to the structure of the problem. In images, language, and speech, expressive models trained with SGD often generalize reliably from finite data.

Algorithmic tasks are different. Examples include:

<ul style="margin: 0; padding-left: 0; list-style-position: inside;">
  <li><strong>Parity:</strong> decide whether \(\langle s, x \rangle \bmod 2 = 1\) for an unknown sparse vector \(s\).</li>
  <li><strong>Primality:</strong> decide whether a large integer is prime.</li>
  <li><strong>Pattern matching:</strong> determine whether a fixed motif appears in a binary string.</li>
  <li><strong>Balanced parentheses:</strong> decide whether a bracket string is a valid Dyck-2 word.</li>
</ul>

These are natural computational problems with compact exact implementations. But when large neural models are trained on labeled examples from such tasks, a consistent failure mode appears.

### The failure mode

Across several 1B-scale language models, training on a few hundred labeled examples yields near-perfect training accuracy on tasks such as parity, Dyck-2, and cellular automata parity. Test accuracy, however, often remains near chance. This behavior can persist even when the amount of training data is increased substantially.

This is not just a matter of architecture choice, hyperparameter tuning, or lack of pretraining. The same qualitative pattern appears across different backbones, broad hyperparameter sweeps, and even strong in-context learning or fine-tuning baselines.

### Why SGD struggles

The statistical query perspective gives a clean explanation. Gradient-based methods access the data distribution only through aggregate statistics, and for families such as sparse parity this creates an inherent bottleneck. Informally, the target rule is simple as a program, but difficult to recover through gradient-based correlation estimates.

For planted \(k\)-parity over \(n\) variables, finite-precision mini-batch coordinate SGD with batch size \(B\) and \(b\)-bit quantization requires at least

$$
q = \Omega\left(\frac{\sqrt{\binom{n}{k}}}{2^b}\right)
$$

fresh examples to achieve nontrivial population error.

So the issue is not that the target rule is intrinsically complicated. The issue is that **SGD is the wrong search procedure for this hypothesis class**.

## Part II: ERM over short programs is statistically attractive

### Think of the target as code

Suppose the target rule can be expressed as a program of length \(L\) over an alphabet \(\Sigma\). Then the relevant hypothesis class is the set of valid programs of length at most \(L\), and its size is bounded by

$$
|\mathcal{H}_L| \le |\Sigma|^L.
$$

This immediately yields a classical PAC-style guarantee. If the shortest consistent program has length \(L\), then with probability at least \(1-\delta\) over the sample,

$$
\mathrm{test\ error}
\le
\frac{L \log |\Sigma| + \log(2L^2/\delta)}{m}.
$$

The important point is that the sample complexity depends on **description length**, not on raw input dimension. A compact parity program can therefore generalize from surprisingly few examples.

### Length-first ERM

The most direct implementation is length-first enumeration: search over all valid programs of length 1, then 2, then 3, and so on, and return the first one that fits the data.

Conceptually, this is appealing. It is simple, exact, and inherits the sample-efficiency guarantees of finite-class ERM.

Computationally, however, it is intractable. With \(|\Sigma| = 128\) and even moderate program length \(L\), the number of candidates scales like \(|\Sigma|^L\), which becomes enormous very quickly.

### The central tension

This yields a sharp distinction between statistical and computational efficiency:

| | **SGD** | **Length-First ERM** |
|---|---|---|
| **Samples for low error** | \(\Omega(n^{k/2})\) | \(O(k \log n)\) |
| **Per-sample compute** | \(O(1)\) | \(\Omega(n^{\Theta(k)})\) |

SGD is computationally cheap but statistically wasteful on these tasks. Program ERM is statistically efficient but computationally intractable.

So the real question is not whether ERM is appealing in principle. It is whether one can keep ERM's statistical advantages while replacing uniform search with something more structured.

## Part III: LLM-PV makes ERM searchable

### From uniform search to proposal priors

The real bottleneck in program ERM is not verification. Given a candidate program, checking whether it fits the data is easy. The difficult part is proposing promising candidates in the first place.

Uniform search over programs of length \(L\) assigns probability roughly \(|\Sigma|^{-L}\) to any fixed target. That is why brute-force ERM is effectively impossible.

A pretrained LLM changes this proposal distribution. Conditioned on labeled examples, it does not generate code uniformly from program space. Instead, it places probability mass on short, structured, plausible rules suggested by the data.

That is the key operational role of the LLM in this framework. It is not the final predictor. It is a proposal mechanism.

### The LLM-PV procedure

LLM-PV, or propose-and-verify, is simple:

<ol style="margin: 0; padding-left: 0; list-style-position: inside;">
  <li><strong>Construct a prompt</strong> from the training examples \(S_{\mathrm{tr}}\).</li>
  <li><strong>Sample</strong> \(k\) candidate programs from the LLM.</li>
  <li><strong>Compile and execute</strong> each candidate in a sandbox.</li>
  <li><strong>Select</strong> the program with the lowest validation error on \(S_{\mathrm{val}}\).</li>
</ol>

No gradient updates are used. The LLM does not itself optimize the learning objective. It only proposes candidate hypotheses. Final selection is still based on held-out empirical error, just as in ERM.

### The formal guarantee

The guarantee cleanly separates the statistical role of validation from the search role of the LLM. Let \(p_\epsilon(S_{\mathrm{tr}})\) denote the probability that a fresh LLM proposal has population error at most \(\epsilon\). Then after \(k\) trials, with probability at least \(1-\delta\),

$$
\mathrm{err}_D(h^\star)
\le
\epsilon + 2\sqrt{\frac{\log(4k/\delta)}{2m_{\mathrm{val}}}}.
$$

The LLM enters only through \(p_\epsilon\), the probability that it proposes a good program. It does not need to be trusted as the final predictor. It only needs to assign enough mass to good hypotheses so that one appears within a manageable number of trials.

In this way, the effective search size is no longer \(|\Sigma|^L\), but roughly \(1/p_\epsilon(S_{\mathrm{tr}})\). Whenever the LLM is substantially better than uniform search, the computational burden drops accordingly.

## The right mental model

The most useful way to view this method is not as "LLMs solve program synthesis," and not as a replacement for standard deep learning on perception tasks.

The picture is narrower and cleaner. Classical ERM over programs is statistically attractive but computationally inaccessible because uniform search is too expensive. A pretrained LLM changes the proposal distribution. It makes some short, structured hypotheses much easier to find. Verification and final selection still come from the data.

So the role of the LLM is not to replace ERM, but to make a previously impractical ERM procedure searchable.

This perspective also explains the limitations. If the target does not admit a short exploitable program, or if the LLM assigns negligible mass to useful candidates, then the method will fail. The SHA-256 result is an example of exactly this regime. In that setting, the problem is not that the framework is incorrectly specified. It is that there is no short compressible structure for the proposal prior to exploit.

<hr class="section-rule">

## What this does and does not say

This argument does **not** say that LLM-PV replaces deep learning on standard vision, language, or audio tasks. In those domains, the usual pipeline remains extremely effective.

What it *does* show is that **classical ERM over programs becomes practically relevant again** when a pretrained LLM provides a strong search prior. The LLM does not replace verification, and it does not change the learning objective. It changes only the proposal mechanism.

More broadly, the method highlights a useful division of labor:

<ul style="margin: 0; padding-left: 0; list-style-position: inside;">
  <li>the <strong>LLM proposes</strong></li>
  <li>the <strong>data verifies</strong></li>
  <li>the <strong>selection rule remains ERM</strong></li>
</ul>

That separation is what makes the approach both effective and auditable.

  <p style="margin-bottom: 1rem;">Pretrained LLMs can make program-based ERM practical by replacing brute-force search with guided search. The mechanism has four parts:</p>

    <li><strong>LLM-PV changes the search distribution.</strong> A pretrained LLM proposes plausible candidate programs, and held-out verification selects the best one, preserving the ERM principle while making the search feasible.</li>
  </ol>
  <p style="margin-top: 1rem; margin-bottom: 0;">The resulting paradigm can recover exact algorithmic rules from a handful of examples, generalize beyond the training distribution, and produce executable hypotheses that can be inspected and verified directly.</p>
